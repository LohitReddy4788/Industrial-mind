INCIDENT REPORT — MECHANICAL SEAL FAILURE WITH HYDROCARBON RELEASE
Incident Reference: IR-2023-012
Date of Incident: 15-March-2023, 02:35 hrs (Night Shift)
Location: Pump House — Crude Transfer Area, Unit 3
Equipment: P-101 (Centrifugal Pump — Crude Oil Transfer)
Shift Incharge: Mr. Vijay Shenoy
Operator on duty: Mr. Arun Patel

INCIDENT DESCRIPTION:
At 02:35 hrs, operator Arun Patel noticed hydrocarbon vapour smell near P-101 during routine rounds. On inspection, found significant crude oil leak from mechanical seal area — estimated 8-12 litres/minute. Activated emergency response immediately.

IMMEDIATE CONSEQUENCES:
- Hydrocarbon pool forming on pump house floor (estimated 200 litres before isolation)
- Fire risk: pump house classified Zone 1 (IEC 60079) — ignition risk HIGH
- Two operators evacuated pump house as precaution
- Emergency isolation completed in 4 minutes from detection
- No ignition, no fire, no injuries
- Environmental: Crude oil contained within bunded area — no drain/soil contamination

TIMELINE:
02:35 — Hydrocarbon smell detected by operator during rounds
02:36 — Shift incharge informed, emergency response activated
02:37 — P-101 isolated (discharge valve closed, pump tripped from DCS)
02:39 — P-102 (standby) started — production continuity maintained
02:40 — Fire tender on standby positioned at pump house entrance
02:55 — Area declared safe for inspection after vapour dispersal
03:15 — Maintenance team mobilized for emergency repair
06:30 — P-101 mechanical seal replaced, pump ready
07:15 — P-101 restarted after testing, P-102 back on standby

ROOT CAUSE ANALYSIS (RCA — 5-Why Method):
Why 1: Why did the mechanical seal fail?
→ Inner seal faces worn beyond limits (face gap >0.05mm)

Why 2: Why were faces worn beyond limits?
→ Barrier fluid (API Plan 53B) pressure had dropped from 12 bar to 3 bar — insufficient face lubrication

Why 3: Why did barrier fluid pressure drop?
→ Bladder accumulator (nitrogen pre-charge) had lost nitrogen charge — bladder failure

Why 4: Why did accumulator bladder fail without detection?
→ Nitrogen pre-charge pressure not included in daily operator rounds checklist

Why 5: Why not in checklist?
→ Seal support system monitoring was excluded from operations checklist during last SOP revision (Feb 2022 revision — documentation error)

ROOT CAUSE: Operational/Procedural — Inadequate monitoring of seal support system (API Plan 53B accumulator pre-charge pressure)
CONTRIBUTING CAUSE: Mechanical — Accumulator bladder natural wear/fatigue (7 years old, OEM replacement interval 5 years — OVERDUE)

CORRECTIVE ACTIONS:
Immediate (Completed within 24 hours):
1. ✅ Mechanical seal replaced with new Flowserve 28AT seal
2. ✅ Accumulator bladder replaced (Bosch Rexroth, new)
3. ✅ Barrier fluid recharged, pre-charge pressure set to 12 bar
4. ✅ Seal support system inspected and flushed

Short-term (within 1 week):
5. ✅ Operator rounds checklist updated — added accumulator pre-charge pressure (check 4-hourly)
6. ✅ SOP-007 revised — added seal support system monitoring section
7. ✅ Toolbox talk conducted for all operators — seal monitoring importance
8. ✅ P-102 (standby pump) seal support system inspected — bladder found at 4 bar (also overdue — replaced preventively)

Long-term (within 1 month):
9. ✅ Installed low-pressure alarm on all Plan 53B accumulators (trip at <8 bar, alarm at <10 bar)
10. ✅ Accumulator bladder replacement added to 5-year PM schedule for all pumps
11. ✅ RCA findings shared plant-wide in monthly safety meeting

REGULATORY COMPLIANCE:
- Incident reported to Factory Inspector within 24 hours as per Section 88, Factories Act 1948 (hydrocarbon release >50kg equivalent)
- OISD-116 safety management audit trail updated
- Insurance notified (HCC Industrial Insurance Policy)

FINANCIAL IMPACT:
- Emergency maintenance cost: ₹1,52,000
- Production loss: NIL (standby pump available)
- Environmental cleanup: ₹18,000
- Total: ₹1,70,000

LESSONS LEARNED:
1. Seal support system health is as critical as the seal itself — must be actively monitored
2. SOP revisions require mandatory review by operations team before approval
3. Accumulator bladders have finite life — must be on PM schedule regardless of appearance
4. Standby pump availability prevented major production impact — standby readiness critical
5. Quick operator response (4 minutes to isolation) prevented escalation to fire/explosion

Similar equipment at risk: P-102, P-103, P-201, P-202 — all checked and updated
